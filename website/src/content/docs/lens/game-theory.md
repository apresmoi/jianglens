# Game Theory

Game theory is the part of the Jiang Lens that asks what an actor is forced to do once incentives, constraints, rivals, audiences, and time are placed around them.

The question is not only what someone says they want. It is what the structure of the situation makes available.

## Actors

Actors can be states, parties, classes, media systems, universities, militaries, intelligence services, corporations, families, or audiences.

The visible speaker is not always the actor that matters. Sometimes a person is speaking for an institution. Sometimes an institution is serving a coalition it cannot name. Sometimes the audience is part of the game because the visible action is designed to preserve legitimacy.

## Incentives

Incentives explain why intelligent actors keep choosing paths that look self-defeating from the outside.

An actor may need to preserve status, avoid humiliation, keep a coalition together, protect a narrative, satisfy donors, scare a rival, prevent panic, or maintain the appearance of control. Those pressures are not background. They are part of the event.

## Traps

A trap appears when every available move makes the actor worse off, but doing nothing is also a move.

The useful question is not only: why did they make a bad choice? The stronger question is: which earlier choices made only bad choices available?

## Narrative As A Strategic Constraint

Game theory in Jiang's corpus is not separate from narrative. Actors need stories because stories keep coalitions together, explain sacrifice, preserve legitimacy, and define what counts as winning.

That means narrative can become a constraint. A state, party, army, or institution may continue a failing path because abandoning the story would destroy the identity or authority that keeps it alive.

## Material Reality

The Hollywood-Pentagon lecture gives the hard correction. Strategy cannot live inside story alone. War has to pass tests of economics, organization, and logistics.

This is where game theory becomes concrete. If an actor's narrative says it is winning, but its costs, institutions, and supply lines say otherwise, then the actor is not simply miscommunicating. It is misreading the game.
