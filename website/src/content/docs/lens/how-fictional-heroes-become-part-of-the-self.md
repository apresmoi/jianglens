# How Fictional Heroes Become Part Of The Self

For Jiang, a fictional hero can become real without having existed as a historical person.

The hero becomes real when the reader finds him inside the self. Achilles is not only a warrior in a distant poem. He becomes a mirror for humiliation, pride, vulnerability, fame, rebellion, and the desire to stand apart from ordinary life.

## The Mirror

The first Homer lecture begins with a practical test: can a student imagine himself as Achilles?

That question changes the status of the poem. Achilles is not a symbol to decode from outside. He is a presence that lets the student feel something in himself. Pride and vulnerability become legible together. Arrogance and insecurity become two sides of the same coin. The character becomes a mirror because the reader recognizes an inner structure through him.

## Consciousness As Self-Distance

The same lecture uses Homer to define consciousness.

To hear yourself speak is to have part of the self step back from the action. One part speaks; another part watches the speaker, watches the listener, and imagines what the words are doing in the listener's mind and heart. Jiang makes this almost ghostly: consciousness is the ability to leave the immediate self and observe the scene from another angle.

Great characters are possible because Homer can do this at scale. He can make Agamemnon, Achilles, Nestor, Odysseus, Hector, Priam, and the surrounding audience live at once.

## The Character Lives In The Reader

The Poets and Prophets lecture gives the stronger claim. Achilles and Odysseus are in the reader, and the reader is in them. Tragedy works because the spectator can see the character from the outside while recognizing the character inside.

That makes literature a technology of interior expansion. The reader gains other selves, other desires, other dangers, and other forms of excellence. A hero does not merely inspire imitation. He becomes a living structure in consciousness.
